# YouTube Ad Creative Frameworks

A comprehensive reference for structuring YouTube in-stream video ads. Use this alongside the script templates to select the right framework for each campaign objective.

---

## The 5-Second Rule

YouTube in-stream ads show a skip button at the 5-second mark. This is the single most important constraint in YouTube ad creative.

**The first 5 seconds are not an introduction. They are your entire pitch in miniature.**

If a viewer skips at 5 seconds, you have failed — even if everything after the 5-second mark was brilliant. The hook must do three things simultaneously:

1. **Hook attention** — interrupt the viewer's passive watching state
2. **Establish relevance** — make the viewer think "this is for me"
3. **Create an open loop** — generate enough curiosity that skipping feels like a loss

### What works in the first 5 seconds

- A specific, surprising number: "£840"
- A direct, uncomfortable question: "Is your boiler older than your car?"
- A visual of a relatable problem — no words needed
- A bold claim with an implied proof: "10,000 families switched last month"
- A short testimonial fragment that creates intrigue

### What never works in the first 5 seconds

- Company logos or brand names (the viewer has no reason to care yet)
- Generic intros: "Hi, welcome to..." / "At [Company], we believe..."
- Fade-ins or animated openers (wastes time, kills momentum)
- Abstract brand statements: "Innovation for a better tomorrow"
- Black screens or slow establishing shots

**Mental test**: Read your first 5 seconds aloud. Does the viewer think "wait, what?" or "that's me"? If not, rewrite the hook.

## Framework 1: Number Hook

**Best for**: Products with quantifiable benefits, cost savings, time savings, efficiency gains

Numbers bypass the brain's language processing and land faster than descriptive words. A specific number in the first frame creates instant curiosity and credibility simultaneously.

### Structure

| Scene | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1. Number Hook | 0-5s | Lead with a surprising stat or number — "£840" / "24 hours" / "97% of homeowners" |
| 2. Context | 5-10s | Explain what the number means — stack the value |
| 3. How It Works | 10-18s | Show the product/service in action — demonstrate the mechanism |
| 4. Risk Removal | 18-23s | Free quote / guarantee / no obligation — remove friction |
| 5. CTA | 23-27s | Direct, clear action — visit URL, call number |

### Example Script (27 seconds)

> **[0-5s]** "£840. That's what the average UK homeowner overpays on energy every year with an old boiler."
>
> **[5-10s]** "But a new A-rated boiler cuts your heating bills by up to 40%. That saving pays for itself."
>
> **[10-18s]** "NOVA connects you with Gas Safe certified engineers who install in under 24 hours — including removing your old boiler."
>
> **[18-23s]** "Free quote. No obligation. Fixed price, no hidden charges."
>
> **[23-27s]** "Visit UKboilers.org today."

### Why it works

The specific number (£840, not "hundreds of pounds") signals real research and triggers loss aversion — the viewer is already losing this money. The context scene converts abstract loss into personal relevance. The mechanism scene answers "how" before the viewer can ask. Risk removal eliminates the last excuse not to act.

### Number selection guidance

- Use the most specific number you can justify: "£847" is more credible than "£850"
- Anchoring works: lead with the larger number (annual cost) before the smaller one (your price)
- Time numbers create urgency: "24 hours", "one day", "this week"
- Social numbers build trust: "10,000 families", "4.8 stars", "over 200 engineers"

## Framework 2: Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)

**Best for**: Services that solve a clear pain point, emergency or time-sensitive services, health and comfort products, any category where the problem is felt but the solution is not yet obvious

People are more motivated by avoiding pain than gaining pleasure. This framework leads with the emotional reality of the problem before offering relief.

### Structure

| Scene | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1. Problem | 0-5s | Show the problem viscerally — the viewer must feel it, not just recognise it |
| 2. Agitate | 5-12s | Twist the knife — show consequences, make it worse before offering the solution |
| 3. Solution Intro | 12-18s | Present the solution as relief — product/service reveal as the answer |
| 4. Proof | 18-23s | Back it up — credentials, stats, social proof, trust signals |
| 5. CTA | 23-27s | Clear next step |

### Example Script (27 seconds)

> **[0-5s]** [Visual: homeowner staring at a boiler making alarming noises, condensation on windows, family in jumpers indoors]
> "That sound your boiler makes? It's not normal."
>
> **[5-12s]** "An old, inefficient boiler doesn't just cost you more every month — as they age, carbon monoxide risk increases. Most homeowners don't know until it's too late."
>
> **[12-18s]** "NOVA connects you with Gas Safe certified engineers for same-day installation. New boiler in, old one out, in under 24 hours."
>
> **[18-23s]** "Rated Excellent on Trustpilot. Over 10,000 installations completed."
>
> **[23-27s]** "Free quote at UKboilers.org."

### Why it works

The problem scene creates emotional identification — the viewer sees their own situation. The agitate phase amplifies the stakes beyond simple inconvenience into genuine risk. This raises the perceived cost of inaction, which is the actual competitor you are fighting. The solution arrives as relief, not a sales pitch.

### Agitation guidelines

- Agitate consequences, not just the problem itself: not "your boiler is old" but "that's money leaving your pocket every month"
- Include the emotional dimension: embarrassment, stress, worry, regret
- Do not exaggerate to the point of incredulity — viewers reject claims that feel manipulative
- The agitate phase should make the viewer want the solution, not just acknowledge the problem exists

## Framework 3: Before-After-Bridge (BAB)

**Best for**: Transformation products, home improvement, lifestyle upgrades, any product where the visual contrast between old and new is striking

Visual contrast is one of the most powerful tools in video advertising. The before/after structure creates an immediate desire for transformation by showing both states in quick succession.

### Structure

| Scene | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1. Before | 0-5s | Show the undesirable current state — relatable, slightly exaggerated, emotionally resonant |
| 2. After | 5-12s | Show the transformed state — aspirational, warm, satisfying |
| 3. Bridge | 12-20s | Product/service is the mechanism that moves someone from before to after |
| 4. Social Proof | 20-24s | Credentials, reviews, trust signals — validate the transformation claim |
| 5. CTA | 24-27s | Clear action step |

### Example Script (27 seconds)

> **[0-5s]** [Cold house in winter. Family in coats indoors. Frost on inside of windows. Old, noisy boiler in a dark cupboard]
> "Still waiting for your home to warm up?"
>
> **[5-12s]** [Same house. Warm amber light. Family comfortable in t-shirts. Sleek new boiler in a clean cupboard. Radiators properly hot]
> "This is what a new boiler actually feels like."
>
> **[12-20s]** "NOVA installs A-rated Martin boilers — same day, Gas Safe certified, fully fitted including removing your old one."
>
> **[20-24s]** "Join 10,000+ happy homeowners. Rated Excellent on Trustpilot."
>
> **[24-27s]** "Get your free quote at UKboilers.org."

### Why it works

The before state creates empathy and identification. The after state creates desire. The bridge (your product) becomes the obvious solution without having to argue for it — the viewer has already felt both states and wants to move from one to the other.

### Before/after visual guidance

- The before and after must be set in the same context (same house, same family) — generic stock of "cold house" vs. "warm house" is less effective
- Exaggerate tastefully: the before should be worse than average, the after should be better than average
- Use colour temperature: cool/blue for before, warm/amber for after — this works subconsciously
- Include people, not just the product — emotional engagement requires human presence

## Framework 4: Social Proof Lead

**Best for**: Established brands with strong reviews, products in competitive markets where differentiation is difficult, switching campaigns, any context where peer behaviour is a primary motivator

Social proof triggers herd behaviour. If thousands of people have done something, the risk of being wrong is perceived as lower. This framework leads with the crowd to reduce perceived risk before presenting the value proposition.

### Structure

| Scene | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1. Social Proof Hook | 0-5s | Open with an impressive and specific stat or testimonial fragment |
| 2. Why They Switched | 5-12s | Explain the value proposition through others' experience |
| 3. How Easy | 12-18s | Show simplicity of process — remove perceived effort barrier |
| 4. Offer | 18-23s | Special offer, guarantee, or incentive — give a reason to act now |
| 5. CTA | 23-27s | Join the crowd — frame the action as joining, not deciding |

### Example Script (27 seconds)

> **[0-5s]** "10,000 UK families upgraded their boiler last month."
>
> **[5-12s]** "They're saving an average of £840 a year on energy — and their homes are warmer from day one."
>
> **[12-18s]** "One call. One day. New boiler, fully fitted, old one removed."
>
> **[18-23s]** "Free quote. No obligation. Fixed price guaranteed."
>
> **[23-27s]** "Visit UKboilers.org — join them."

### Why it works

"10,000 families" frames the decision as already made by a large reference group. The viewer is not being asked to take a risk — they are being offered the chance to do what everyone else is already doing. The final line ("join them") reinforces this framing deliberately.

### Social proof selection guidance

- Specific numbers outperform vague claims: "10,000 families" beats "thousands of families"
- Review platform mentions add credibility: "Rated Excellent on Trustpilot" beats "loved by customers"
- Recency matters: "last month" and "this year" signal momentum
- Endorsements work: Gas Safe certification, Which? approval, energy rating bodies
- Testimonial fragments can work as social proof: a real person's quote, even a few words, beats a stat

## Framework 5: Question Hook

**Best for**: Awareness campaigns, products people did not know they needed, educational or category-creating content, re-engagement of dormant interest

Questions create an open loop in the viewer's mind. The brain is wired to seek closure on open questions — a well-phrased question makes skipping feel uncomfortable because the answer is still missing.

### Structure

| Scene | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1. Question | 0-5s | Ask an irresistible, personally relevant question |
| 2. Answer | 5-10s | Provide the surprising or useful answer — earn the viewer's trust with genuine information |
| 3. Consequence | 10-16s | Show what this means specifically for the viewer — personalise the stakes |
| 4. Solution | 16-22s | Product/service as the natural answer to the implied problem |
| 5. CTA | 22-27s | Take action now |

### Example Script (27 seconds)

> **[0-5s]** "Is your boiler older than your car?"
>
> **[5-10s]** "Boilers over 10 years old waste up to 40% of the energy they use — just generating heat that disappears through the pipes."
>
> **[10-16s]** "That's around £840 out of your pocket every year. And the older they get, the worse it becomes."
>
> **[16-22s]** "A new Martin A-rated boiler pays for itself in energy savings. Installed in one day by Gas Safe engineers."
>
> **[22-27s]** "Free quote at UKboilers.org."

### Why it works

"Is your boiler older than your car?" is personal and slightly uncomfortable. Most people know roughly how old their boiler is, and most people's cars are newer than their boilers. The question creates instant self-evaluation — the viewer is already thinking about their boiler before the second line arrives.

### Question selection guidance

- The question must be answerable by the viewer in their head immediately: "Is your boiler older than your car?" works; "Do you know your EPC rating?" is too specific
- The implied answer should be uncomfortable: the best question hooks already suggest the viewer has a problem
- Avoid rhetorical questions that feel like ad-speak: "Are you tired of high energy bills?" triggers skip behaviour
- "Did you know...?" works when the fact that follows is genuinely surprising and relevant

## Framework Selection Guide

Use this table when briefing a campaign to select the starting framework. Multiple frameworks may be tested as creative variants.

| Goal | Best Framework | Why |
|------|---------------|-----|
| Drive immediate action / response | Number Hook | Concrete value proposition creates fast logical buy-in |
| Emergency or urgent services | PAS | Amplifies urgency and makes inaction feel dangerous |
| Home improvement / lifestyle upgrade | BAB | Visual transformation creates desire without argument |
| Competitive market / switching campaigns | Social Proof Lead | Reduces perceived risk of changing supplier or brand |
| Low awareness / category education | Question Hook | Creates curiosity without requiring prior interest |
| High-ticket items requiring justification | PAS or BAB | Emotional buy-in needed to justify larger purchase |
| Impulse / low-consideration purchase | Number Hook | Fast value calculation enables fast decision |
| Retargeting warm audiences | Social Proof Lead | Reinforces that others have made the same decision |
| Brand awareness (not direct response) | Question Hook or BAB | Builds association and desire without hard sell |

### When to test multiple frameworks

Always test at least two frameworks when:
- Launching a new campaign (no historical data to guide selection)
- Targeting a cold audience (unknown emotional triggers)
- Running significant budget (the cost of testing is justified by the optimisation upside)

Structure A/B tests with a single variable — same offer, same CTA, different hook framework. This isolates which framework resonates with the specific audience segment.

## VO Pacing Guidelines

Voiceover pacing is the most commonly miscalibrated element in YouTube ad scripts. Too fast and the viewer cannot process the information. Too slow and the viewer skips before the message lands.

### Word count targets by runtime

| Runtime | Word Count | Pacing Notes |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| 15s (bumper) | 30-40 words | Fast, punchy, no pauses — every word must earn its place |
| 20s (short skippable) | 45-55 words | Brisk and clear, minimal pauses, rhythm matters |
| 27s (standard skippable) | 60-75 words | Conversational pace, natural pauses between sections |
| 30s (standard) | 70-85 words | Comfortable, room for emphasis and variation |
| 60s (long-form) | 140-170 words | Storytelling pace, emotional beats, can afford silence |

**Rule of thumb**: 2.5 to 3 words per second for natural-sounding VO. Faster for urgency, slower for premium or high-trust contexts.

### Strategic pause placement

Pauses are not wasted time — they are processing time. Place pauses:

- **After the hook** — let the number or question land before moving on
- **Before the CTA** — a half-beat of silence builds micro-anticipation
- **Between major value props** — give each claim room to register before the next arrives

A script that sounds breathless when read aloud will feel overwhelming when heard. Read every script aloud and time it before signing off.

### VO tone guidance by framework

| Framework | VO Tone |
|-----------|---------|
| Number Hook | Confident, authoritative, matter-of-fact — no hype |
| PAS | Empathetic first, then urgent — never alarmist |
| BAB | Warm and aspirational — narrative storytelling pace |
| Social Proof Lead | Assured, understated — the numbers speak, VO should not oversell |
| Question Hook | Conversational, curious — the VO is asking the viewer a genuine question |

## Scene Timing Best Practices

### First frame

The first frame is not a title card. It is your visual hook. It must:

- Be visually striking from the first pixel — no fade-ins, no black screens
- Establish context immediately — viewer should know what they are watching within 1 second
- Contain visual tension or contrast — something that makes the eye stay

For product ads: show the problem, not the product. The product earns its reveal.
For testimonial-led ads: start on the person's face mid-sentence, not an intro graphic.
For number hooks: the number should appear as text overlay on a relevant visual — not on a blank background.

### Timing discipline

| Phase | Timing | Rule |
|-------|--------|------|
| Hook only | 0-5s | No logos, no brand names, no context-setting — hook only |
| Core message | 5-15s | Value prop, mechanism, and proof compete for this window |
| Risk removal | 15-22s | Guarantee, free quote, no obligation — remove the last friction |
| CTA | Last 3-5s | One action, one URL or one phone number, nothing else |

### Transition timing

- Keep transitions to 0.3 to 0.5 seconds — viewers do not wait for slow dissolves
- Cut transitions are preferred for fast-paced frameworks (Number Hook, PAS)
- Soft cuts or brief dissolves work for BAB when moving from before to after state
- Never use animated wipes or branded transition effects — they signal amateur production

### Scene change frequency

Change scenes every 3 to 5 seconds to maintain pacing. Viewer attention in YouTube ads decays rapidly — a single shot held for 10 seconds will lose viewers even if the content is strong.

### VeO clip strategy

VeO-generated clips are 8 seconds each. Plan to use 3 to 5 seconds from each clip — the strongest portion. Approach clip planning knowing you will trim heavily:

- Brief the clip for what it needs to show, not how long you need it
- Generate more clips than required and select the strongest takes
- Plan your cuts before generating — know which 3-5 seconds you want from each 8-second clip
- The best portion of a VeO clip is usually the middle 4 seconds (skip the ramp-in and tail)

## Quick Reference Card

### Framework selection in 10 seconds

1. Is there a striking number? → **Number Hook**
2. Is there a clear, felt pain? → **PAS**
3. Is the visual transformation compelling? → **BAB**
4. Is there strong social proof to lead with? → **Social Proof Lead**
5. Would a question make the viewer self-evaluate? → **Question Hook**

### Non-negotiable rules

- Never waste the first 5 seconds on logos or brand names
- Every script must be read aloud and timed before finalising
- Word count must match the runtime target within 10%
- First frame must be visually striking — no black screens, no fade-ins
- CTA is one action only — one URL or one phone number, never both
- Change scenes at minimum every 5 seconds

### The skip test

Before finalising any script, ask: if the viewer skips at exactly 5 seconds, have they received a complete, valuable micro-message? The first 5 seconds should work as a standalone impression even if nothing else is seen.
