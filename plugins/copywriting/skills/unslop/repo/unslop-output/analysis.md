## 2. SENTENCE-LEVEL STRUCTURAL PATTERNS

### 2.1 The "Whether X or Y" construction
Used to span the range of customer situations in a single sentence, almost always with two scenarios.
- "Whether your boiler's packed in on a January morning or you need a landlord gas safety certificate" — sample 1
- "Whether your boiler is six months old or six years old" — sample 11
- **Count: 9/30**

### 2.2 The em-dash differentiator ("X — not Y")
Used to contrast the business against a negative alternative, usually a call centre or national company.
- "We're local Manchester engineers — not a national call centre" — sample 0
- "Family-run, Bristol-based — You'll speak to us, not a call handler" — sample 12
- **Count: 14/30**

### 2.3 Short declarative follow-up after a longer sentence
A rhetorical device where a longer explanatory sentence is followed by a very short (3–7 word) punchy sentence.
- "So I left." — sample 4
- "That's what we do." — sample 28
- "We don't subcontract." — sample 20
- "Bristol is home." — sample 12
- **Count: 9/30**

### 2.4 Tricolon of negatives ("No X. No Y. No Z.")
- "No fuss, no inflated quotes, no subcontractors." — sample 1

- "No heat. No hot water. Sometimes both at once" — sample 16
- "No call-out fee. No surprise quotes on the doorstep." — sample 16

## 11. TOP 10 SLOP PATTERNS TO AVOID

Ranked by combination of frequency and genericness:

1. **"Gas Safe registered" as the default closing trust anchor** (27/30) — used so universally it has zero differentiation value; every competitor uses it identically
2. **"Fixed prices, no surprises" pricing reassurance formula** (17/30) — near-identical wording across unrelated businesses
3. **"Honest" as a personality claim without supporting evidence** (16/30) — asserted repeatedly, never demonstrated through specifics
4. **Fear-then-reassure hero structure** (16/30) — problem sentence + "we'll sort it" sentence is the automatic default
5. **"No call centre" differentiator** (10/30) — identical framing used by every business claiming to be local
6. **"In plain English, no jargon"** (12/30) — exact phrase recycled unchanged across all service types
7. **"Sorted" as the resolution verb** (12/30) — colloquial but overused as CTA/hero default
8. **Tricolon of negatives "No X. No Y. No Z."** (11/30) — industry-refusal list always follows the same three-beat pattern
9. **The "Whether X or Y" sentence** (9/30) — spanning two customer scenarios as a structural crutch
10. **"Here's the [content], ready to [action]" meta-framing opener** (19/30) — AI delivery language that exposes the generation process rather than just delivering the content
