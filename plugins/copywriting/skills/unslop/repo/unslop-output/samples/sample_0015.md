# Central Heating Installation: Everything You Need in Your New Home

## You've got the keys. Now let's make it a home you can actually live in.

Buying your first property is one of the biggest things you'll ever do. So finding out it has no central heating — no boiler, no radiators, no hot water — can feel like a problem you didn't budget for and don't quite know how to tackle.

You're not alone. Thousands of UK properties, particularly older terraced houses, flats, and rural homes, still rely on storage heaters, back boilers, or nothing at all. Installing central heating from scratch is entirely normal, and with the right heating engineer it's a far less stressful process than most people expect.

This page explains exactly what's involved, what affects the cost, and what you should expect from start to finish — in plain English, without the technical jargon.

## What a Full Central Heating Installation Includes

A complete central heating installation isn't just a boiler on the wall. It's an integrated system, and every component needs to work together properly.

**The Boiler** — The heart of the system. Three main types:
- **Combi boiler** — Heats water on demand, no cylinder needed. Most popular for two- and three-bedroom homes.
- **System boiler** — Works with a hot water cylinder; better for homes with multiple bathrooms or higher demand.
- **Heat-only (conventional)** — Traditional setup with cylinder and cold water tank. Less common in new installs but right for certain properties.

**Radiators** — Sized and positioned based on a proper heat loss calculation for each room. Every radiator gets a thermostatic radiator valve (TRV) for room-by-room temperature control.

**Pipework** — Copper pipework runs throughout the property connecting boiler to radiators and hot water outlets. Run beneath floorboards or within walls where possible to minimise disruption.

**Controls and Thermostat** — A programmer or smart thermostat (Nest, Hive, etc.) lets you set schedules and manage the system remotely. Smart controls can meaningfully reduce energy bills over time.

**Hot Water Cylinder (if applicable)** — Required for system and heat-only boilers. Usually installed in an airing cupboard, storing pre-heated water ready on demand.

## Why Installing Before You Move In Makes Sense

**The property is empty.** Engineers can lift floorboards and work freely through every room without working around your furniture or your daily life. Faster, neater, less disruptive.

**No upheaval once you're living there.** An installation creates dust and some mess. When you're not living in it, this simply doesn't affect you.

**You move into a warm, comfortable home.** A significant difference from moving in and spending weeks waiting.

**Decorate after, not before.** Pipework and radiators go in first, then you paint — not the other way around.

## What Affects the Cost

- **Property size** — More rooms mean more radiators and pipework
- **Boiler type and brand** — Wide range of quality and efficiency levels
- **Pipework access** — Solid floors take longer than suspended timber; complex layouts add labour
- **Number of radiators** — Based on rooms and heat loss calculation
- **Hot water cylinder** — Required for system/heat-only boilers, adds to scope
- **Condition of gas supply** — Meter upgrades or gas pipe replacement where needed

A reputable engineer surveys the property before quoting, so you understand exactly what's included and why.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does it take?**
Two to four days for most two- or three-bedroom homes. Larger properties or solid floors can take up to five days. Your engineer will give a realistic timeline at survey stage.

**Do I need to be there?**
Not necessarily. Many customers hand over keys while the property is empty. Your engineer will keep you updated and contact you if any decisions arise.

**Will it be messy?**
Some disruption — floorboards lifted, occasional wall work. Engineers use dustsheets and leave the property tidy at the end of each day. Most customers are pleasantly surprised.

**Which boiler should I choose?**
For most one-bathroom homes, a combi is the practical choice. Two or more bathrooms, or high hot water demand, often suits a system boiler better. Your engineer will recommend based on your specific property.

**Do I need planning permission?**
No. Building Regulations compliance (Part L) is required but handled by your registered engineer as part of the job.

**What if the property has no gas connection?**
Options include oil-fired heating, a heat pump, or LPG where mains gas isn't available. We advise on all of these at survey stage.
