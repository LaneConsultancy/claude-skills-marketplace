# Snowball Content Method

Turn one core problem into a full month of content. The method works by systematic expansion: one problem → multiple angles → subtopics per angle → format variants per subtopic. This creates coherent, interconnected content that builds topical authority rather than scattered one-off posts.

## Why this works better than brainstorming

The natural instinct is to ask "give me 20 post ideas." The problem: you get 20 disconnected topics, none of which build on each other. Audiences don't follow accounts that post about random things — they follow accounts that go deep on problems they care about. The Snowball Method forces depth over breadth, and every piece of content reinforces the others.

## The Process

This skill runs in four phases. Phases 1-2 are conversational (ask questions, get answers, refine). Phases 3-4 produce files. Do not skip ahead — each phase depends on the one before it.

### Phase 1: Discovery

Before generating anything, you need to understand the business. Ask the user these questions (adapt wording to sound natural, don't read them like a form):

**Required:**
- What's the business / what do you sell or do?
- Who are you trying to reach? (Be specific — not "homeowners" but "homeowners whose boiler just broke at 10pm")
- What platforms are you active on or want to be? (LinkedIn, Instagram, YouTube, Facebook, TikTok, X, email newsletter, blog — get specific)
- What's the goal? (Leads, bookings, brand awareness, email signups, community building)

**Useful but not blocking:**
- What do your customers complain about or ask most often?
- What do competitors in your space all say the same way?
- Any topics that are off-limits or sensitive in your industry?
- What tone fits? (Professional, casual, funny, authoritative, educational)

If the user has already provided this context (e.g. from memory or earlier in the conversation), don't re-ask what you already know. Fill gaps only.

Once you have enough, synthesise into the **Core Brief**:

```
CORE BRIEF
───────────
Business:       [what they do]
Audience:       [who, specifically]
Platforms:      [where they'll post]
Goal:           [what success looks like]
Tone:           [how they want to sound]
Constraints:    [anything off-limits]
```

Present this back to the user and confirm before moving on.

### Phase 2: Core Problem + ICP

This is the strategic heart of the method. From the discovery answers, identify ONE core problem in the user's niche that meets all three criteria:

1. **Their audience actually experiences it** (not a theoretical problem)
2. **People are willing to discuss and share content about it** (it has social currency — it's relatable, surprising, or emotionally charged)
3. **The business can credibly speak to it** (they have real experience or expertise)

Present the core problem with:
- **Why this problem matters** — 2-3 sharp sentences. No fluff.
- **30-day content goal** — What are we measuring? (Saves, leads, DMs, email signups, shares — pick 1-2 that match their stated goal)
- **ICP Table:**

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Who they are | [specific description] |
| What they want | [desired outcome] |
| What blocks them | [obstacles, fears, misconceptions] |
| Language they use | [actual phrases, not marketing speak] |
| Triggers | [moments when they'd search or engage] |
| Taboos | [what turns them off, what feels salesy or condescending] |

Get user confirmation on the core problem before expanding. If they push back, iterate — don't steamroll.

### Phase 3: Snowball Expansion

This is where one topic becomes many. The expansion follows a strict hierarchy:

```
Core Problem
├── Angle 1 (unexpected perspective)
│   ├── Subtopic 1a
│   │   ├── Format: Educational
│   │   ├── Format: Provocative
│   │   └── Format: Case Study
│   ├── Subtopic 1b
│   │   └── ...
│   └── Subtopic 1c
│       └── ...
├── Angle 2
│   └── ...
└── Angle N
    └── ...
```

#### Step 1: Generate Angles

Propose **5-8 angles** on the core problem. Each angle must be a genuinely different perspective, not a rephrasing. Use these lens types to ensure variety:

| Lens | What it does | Example (for "boiler breakdowns") |
|------|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Counterintuitive | Flips common wisdom | "Why servicing your boiler might be costing you more" |
| Myth vs Reality | Busts misconceptions | "The British Gas engineer myth" |
| Hidden Cost | Reveals what people don't calculate | "What a cold shower actually costs your morning" |
| "You're doing it wrong" | Challenges current behaviour | "Stop bleeding your radiators — here's why" |
| Behind the scenes | Shows the reality of the work | "What your heating engineer won't tell you" |
| Comparison | Frames a choice people face | "Repair vs replace: the maths nobody does" |
| Timely/Seasonal | Ties to a moment | "September: the month that breaks boilers" |
| Emotional/Story | Personal or customer narrative | "The worst callout I've ever had" |

For each angle, provide:
- **2 hooks** (≤12 words each) — these are potential opening lines or headlines
- **Core insight** (≤80 words) — the actual point being made
- **Credibility anchor** — a number, fact, or specific detail that makes it believable
- **Spicy take** — one statement designed to spark comments or disagreement

No overlap between angles. If two angles make the same point from slightly different positions, merge them or cut one.

#### Step 2: Expand to Subtopics

For each angle, generate **2-4 subtopics**. Each subtopic should provoke a "huh, I never thought of it that way" reaction. Subtopics are more specific slices of the angle — they're what actually become individual posts.

#### Step 3: Format Variants

For each subtopic, create briefs in three formats. These are content briefs, not finished posts — enough for the user (or a writer) to execute.

**Educational:**
- Hook (opening line)
- Core insight (the thing they'll learn)
- 3 actionable steps ("do this today")
- CTA

**Provocative:**
- Debatable hook (designed to make people react)
- 3 supporting points
- Closing question (to drive comments)

**Case Study:**
- Context (situation before)
- Action (what was done)
- Numbers (specific results)
- Takeaway (the lesson)

Not every subtopic needs all three formats. Use judgement — if a subtopic doesn't have real numbers for a case study, skip it. Flag which formats work best for which platforms based on the user's platform list.

#### Platform Adaptation Notes

For each content brief, add a short note on how it should adapt per platform. Don't write separate versions — just note the key differences:

- **LinkedIn:** Longer form OK, professional framing, hooks matter enormously (first 2 lines before "see more")
- **Instagram (feed/carousel):** Visual-first, break into slides, strong opening slide
- **Instagram (Reels) / TikTok:** 15-60 seconds, hook in first 2 seconds, show don't tell
- **YouTube:** Longer form, thumbnail + title are the hook, structure matters
- **Facebook:** Community-oriented, questions work well, shares > likes
- **X / Twitter:** Punchy, thread format for depth, hot takes perform
- **Email newsletter:** Can go deeper, personal tone, exclusive angle
- **Blog:** SEO-friendly, comprehensive, evergreen

Only include platforms the user is actually using.

### Phase 4: Calendar + Deliverables

Now assemble everything into two files.

#### Strategy Document (.docx)

Read the docx skill at `/mnt/skills/public/docx/SKILL.md` before creating this file.

Structure:
1. **Core Brief** (from Phase 1)
2. **Core Problem & ICP** (from Phase 2)
3. **Angle Breakdown** — each angle with its hooks, insights, credibility anchors, and spicy takes
4. **Content Briefs** — organised by angle → subtopic → format, with platform notes
5. **Measurement Notes** — what to track and what "good" looks like for their goal

#### Content Calendar (.xlsx)

Read the xlsx skill at `/mnt/skills/public/xlsx/SKILL.md` before creating this file.

Create a spreadsheet with these columns:
- **Day** (1-30)
- **Date** (if they give a start date; otherwise leave as Day 1, Day 2...)
- **Platform**
- **Angle** (which of the angles this comes from)
- **Subtopic**
- **Format** (Educational / Provocative / Case Study)
- **Hook** (the opening line)
- **Goal** (what this post should achieve — saves, shares, leads, comments)
- **Distribution Notes** (cross-post? boost? share to stories? email tie-in?)

Calendar assembly rules:
- Don't front-load all content from one angle — interleave them
- Provocative content early in the week (Mon-Tue) when engagement is higher
- Educational content mid-week
- Story/case study content toward the weekend
- Build in 2-3 "rest" days or repurpose days (mark as "Repurpose: [original post] as [new format]")
- If they're on multiple platforms, stagger — don't post the same thing everywhere on the same day

Present both files to the user.

## Important Reminders

- **This skill plans content. It does not write finished posts.** The output is briefs and a calendar. If the user wants full posts written, that's a separate task after this one.
- **One core problem per run.** If the user wants to cover multiple topics, run the snowball method separately for each. Mixing topics defeats the purpose.
- **Push back on weak core problems.** If the user suggests a topic that doesn't meet the three criteria (audience experiences it, shareable, business can speak to it), say so and suggest alternatives. Being agreeable here produces bad content plans.
- **The ICP table is not optional.** It shapes every angle and hook. Don't skip it to save time.
- **Adapt the numbers, preserve the hierarchy.** Maybe 5 angles is right instead of 7. Maybe 2 subtopics per angle, not 3. That's fine. But the hierarchy (problem → angles → subtopics → formats) is non-negotiable — it's what makes the method work.
