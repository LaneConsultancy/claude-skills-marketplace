# The Six Lead Types

From "Great Leads" by Michael Masterson — the first 100-300 words determine 80% of your copy's success.

## How to Choose Your Lead

**Direct Leads** work when:
- Reader already knows they have a problem
- Reader is familiar with solutions like yours
- You have a compelling offer or clear advantage
- Trust is already established (return visitors, referrals)

**Indirect Leads** work when:
- Reader doesn't know they need you yet
- Reader is sceptical of service providers
- You need to educate before you can sell
- You're competing against established players

---

## Direct Lead Types

### 1. The Offer Lead

Lead with the deal. Best for high-awareness audiences ready to take action.

**When to use:**
- Special promotions or discounts
- Clear, easy-to-understand offers
- Audiences who already trust you
- Time-sensitive opportunities

**Structure:**
1. State the offer immediately
2. Emphasise the value (what they get)
3. Add urgency or scarcity if genuine
4. Provide a reason-why for the offer

**Example:**
> **Free boiler service when you sign up for our annual cover plan**
> 
> This month, new Boiler Cover customers get their first service included — that's a £95 saving. I'm running this offer because January is traditionally a quieter month, and I'd rather spend that time looking after new customers than sitting idle.
> 
> The plan covers all call-outs, parts, and labour for a full year. No hidden costs, no excess to pay if something goes wrong.

---

### 2. The Promise Lead

Lead with the primary benefit. Best when you can make a bold, believable claim.

**When to use:**
- You have a clear competitive advantage
- The benefit is highly desirable
- You can back the promise with proof

**Structure:**
1. Open with the biggest benefit
2. Make it specific and concrete
3. Connect to what the reader actually wants
4. Transition to proof/credibility

**Key principle:** The promise must resonate with the reader's core desire, not just describe your service.

**Example:**
> **Get your heating fixed the same day — or the call-out is free**
> 
> When your boiler breaks down in December, you can't wait three days for an engineer. That's why I guarantee same-day repairs for all call-outs received before 12pm.
> 
> In the last 12 months, I've hit this target 94% of the time. The 6% who had to wait were all complex jobs requiring specialist parts — and they didn't pay a penny for the call-out.

---

### 3. The Problem-Solution Lead

Start with the pain, then reveal the fix. Best when the problem is emotionally charged.

**When to use:**
- Reader is actively experiencing a problem
- The problem has real consequences
- You have a clear solution
- Empathy builds trust in your niche

**Structure:**
1. Describe the problem vividly (show you understand)
2. Acknowledge the emotional impact
3. Hint at the solution
4. Transition to how you solve it

**Example:**
> **Waking up to a cold house in January isn't just uncomfortable — it's stressful**
> 
> You're wondering if this is a simple fix or a £2,000 replacement. You're trying to find an engineer who can come today, not next week. You're worried about the cost, the disruption, and whether you can trust whoever turns up.
> 
> I've been fixing boilers in Kent for 15 years. In most cases, I can diagnose the problem in 20 minutes, give you a fixed-price quote on the spot, and have your heating back on the same day.

## Indirect Lead Types

### 4. The Secret Lead

Tease exclusive knowledge. Best for building curiosity and positioning expertise.

**When to use:**
- You have insider knowledge readers value
- Readers don't know what they don't know
- You need to establish authority
- Content/education-focused pages

**Structure:**
1. Hint at something most people don't know
2. Explain why this matters to them
3. Build anticipation without revealing everything
4. Transition to your expertise/solution

**Rules:**
- The secret must be genuinely valuable
- Don't reveal it too early (maintain curiosity)
- Don't make it feel clickbait-y

**Example:**
> **The one question most homeowners forget to ask their boiler engineer**
> 
> When your boiler breaks, you ask about the cost. You ask how long it'll take. You might even ask about guarantees. But there's one question that could save you hundreds of pounds — and almost nobody thinks to ask it.
> 
> After 15 years of fixing boilers in Kent, I've seen homeowners make the same expensive mistake over and over...

---

### 5. The Proclamation Lead

Make a bold statement that demands attention. Best for challenging assumptions.

**When to use:**
- You have a contrarian or surprising viewpoint
- Industry norms deserve to be challenged
- You want to stand out from competitors
- The proclamation is genuinely true and provable

**Structure:**
1. Open with a bold, attention-grabbing statement
2. Back it up immediately with evidence
3. Explain the implications for the reader
4. Transition to your approach

**Example:**
> **Most boiler cover plans are designed to make money, not to help you**
> 
> That's not cynicism — it's maths. The big providers charge £15-20/month, set caps on call-outs, exclude common parts, and add excess fees every time you claim. They're betting you won't need them.
> 
> I run my cover plan differently. One price. No excess. No caps. No excluded parts. If something goes wrong, I fix it — that's the deal.

---

### 6. The Story Lead

Draw readers in with narrative. Best for building emotional connection.

**When to use:**
- You have a compelling origin story or customer story
- You need to build trust with sceptical readers
- The story illustrates your value better than claims
- Longer-form content (about pages, blog posts)

**Structure:**
1. Start in the middle of action or conflict
2. Create identification (reader sees themselves)
3. Build tension or curiosity
4. Resolve with your product/service as the solution

**Example:**
> **Last February, I got a call at 6:30am from a family in Dartford**
> 
> Their boiler had failed overnight. The kids were getting dressed under duvets. Dad was on hold with British Gas, being told the earliest slot was Thursday — four days away.
> 
> I was there by 8am. It turned out to be a failed diverter valve — common problem, £180 including parts. By 9:30, the heating was on and the kids were complaining it was too warm.
> 
> That's what local should mean.

## Matching Leads to Page Types

| Page Type | Best Lead Types |
|-----------|----------------|
| Homepage | Promise, Problem-Solution |
| Service pages | Problem-Solution, Promise |
| Offer/promo pages | Offer, Promise |
| About page | Story |
| Blog posts | Secret, Proclamation, Story |
| Landing pages | Depends on traffic source awareness |

## The Lead Checklist

Before publishing, verify your lead:

- [ ] Does it capture attention in the first sentence?
- [ ] Is it one clear idea (Rule of One)?
- [ ] Does it match the reader's awareness level?
- [ ] Does it make a specific promise or provoke curiosity?
- [ ] Does it naturally lead into the rest of the copy?
- [ ] Would you keep reading if you were the target customer?
