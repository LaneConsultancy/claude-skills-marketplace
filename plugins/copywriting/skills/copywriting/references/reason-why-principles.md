# Reason-Why Advertising Principles

Core principles from John E. Kennedy's "Reason Why Advertising" and Claude Hopkins' "Scientific Advertising" — the foundation of all effective copywriting.

## The Central Principle

**Advertising is salesmanship in print.**

Nothing more. Nothing less. If your copy wouldn't work if spoken by a salesman standing in front of a customer, it won't work in print either.

The only difference: copy must be STRONGER and MORE CONVINCING than verbal salesmanship because there's no personal influence, no facial expressions, no ability to respond to objections in real-time.

## What General Publicity Gets Wrong

Most advertising is "General Publicity" — keeping the name before the people, creating impressions, influencing sales. This is waste.

**Signs your copy is General Publicity (not salesmanship):**
- It could run for any competitor without changing the claims
- It doesn't give specific reasons to choose you over alternatives
- It's entertaining but doesn't compel action
- The measure of success is "awareness" rather than sales

**Signs your copy is Salesmanship-in-Print:**
- It makes specific, provable claims
- It gives concrete reasons-why
- It could only work for your product/service
- Success is measured by inquiries and sales

## The Reason-Why Test

For every claim, ask: **"Why should they believe this?"**

Don't answer with more claims. Answer with evidence:

| Weak Claim | Reason-Why Version |
|------------|-------------------|
| "Expert technicians" | "Gas Safe registered with 15+ years' experience" |
| "Fast response" | "Average arrival time of 47 minutes in the local area" |
| "Affordable prices" | "Fixed-price service at £85, no hidden charges" |
| "Quality workmanship" | "12-month guarantee on all work — if anything goes wrong, I'll fix it free" |

## Being Specific

Generalities roll off the mind like water off a duck. Specifics stick.

**Hopkins:** "A man who makes a specific claim is either telling the truth or a lie. People do not expect an advertiser to lie."

**Examples:**
- "Multiplies itself in lather 250 times" beats "Abundant lather"
- "Softens the beard in one minute" beats "Acts quickly"
- "Maintains its creamy fullness for ten minutes on the face" beats "Does not dry out"

**The specificity principle for local businesses:**
- Name specific areas you serve
- Quote actual response times from your records
- Reference real customer reviews by name (with permission)
- State exact prices, not "competitive" or "affordable"

## The Responsive Chord

Different audiences respond to different arguments presented in different ways. The same logical argument can fail or succeed depending on how it's framed.

**Kennedy:** "A given argument, presented in a certain form of thought and expression, will strike responsively in the minds of a given number."

For local service businesses, the responsive chords typically include:
- **Reliability:** "I'll be there when I say I will"
- **Trust:** "I'm a local tradesman, not a faceless company"
- **Expertise:** "I've seen this problem hundreds of times"
- **Safety:** "Your family's safety is my priority"
- **Value:** "One fair price, no surprises"

## The Mail-Order Test

Apply mail-order thinking to all copy. If your copy couldn't sell directly by mail, it's not strong enough.

**Mail-order advertising teaches:**
- Every word must earn its place (space costs money)
- Headlines that don't sell waste everything that follows
- Complete stories sell more than abbreviated ones
- Specifics outperform generalities every time
- "Catchy" copy that doesn't convert is worthless

## Copy Length

**Hopkins:** "The only readers we get are people whom our subject interests. No one reads ads for amusement. Consider them as prospects standing before you, seeking information. Give them enough to get action."

Short copy isn't better than long copy. The right length is however long it takes to:
1. Capture attention
2. Build interest
3. Create desire
4. Overcome objections
5. Compel action

For simple, well-known services: shorter
For complex, high-value, or unfamiliar services: longer

## Conviction Over Attention

**Kennedy:** "The 'seeing,' 'admiring,' or 'reading with interest' of an advertisement avails little in dollars and cents if it fails to CONVINCE."

Your copy can be:
- Seen by thousands
- Admired as clever
- Read with interest
- Shared on social media

And still fail completely if it doesn't CONVINCE people to take action.

**The priority order:**
1. Convince (create belief)
2. Persuade (create desire)
3. Interest (create attention)

Most copy inverts this, chasing attention without building conviction.

## Testing Everything

**Hopkins:** "Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly, and finally, by a test campaign. Go to the court of last resort — the buyers of your product."

For web copy:
- Track which pages convert to enquiries
- A/B test headlines when possible
- Monitor which calls-to-action get clicks
- Ask new customers how they found you and what convinced them

Don't trust opinions (yours or anyone else's). Trust results.
