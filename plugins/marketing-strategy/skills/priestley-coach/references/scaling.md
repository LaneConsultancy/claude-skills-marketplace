# Scaling & Building Business Value

This reference covers Priestley's frameworks for building a business that isn't dependent on you, creating lasting value, and structuring for freedom.

## The 24 Assets Framework

From 24 Assets. The core insight: income follows assets. Struggling businesses chase revenue directly. Successful businesses build assets and revenue follows.

**Definition of a business asset:** Anything that would still be valuable if you and your team disappeared for 90 days. If it requires you to be there to explain it or operate it, it's not an asset yet. The knowledge in your head has potential to be an asset, but until it's documented, productised, or systemised, it isn't one.

### The 7 asset categories (24 assets total)

#### 1. Intellectual Property Assets
Your unique ideas, methods, and legally protectable creations.

- **Content** — Evergreen text, video, audio, images that demonstrate expertise and attract audience. Must be high quality and consistently produced.
- **Methodology** — Your formalised, named process for delivering results. Documented as diagrams, frameworks, or software. This is often the most valuable hidden asset in a business.
- **Registered IP** — Trademarks, patents, domain names, social media handles. Legal protection of your brand and innovations.

#### 2. Brand Assets
How the market perceives and connects with your business.

- **Brand identity** — Visual consistency, tone of voice, values. A recognisable, professional presence across all touchpoints.
- **Key person of influence** — The human face of the brand. Someone known, liked, and trusted who attracts attention and creates connection. (Links directly to the KPI method.)
- **Brand strategy** — Long-term vision, positioning, and the philosophy your brand stands for. What bigger idea does your business represent beyond its products?

#### 3. Market Assets
Your connection to and reach within your target market.

- **Database/audience** — Your list of contacts, subscribers, followers. The people you can reach directly without paying for advertising.
- **Distribution channels** — How your content and products reach the market. Social platforms, email, partnerships, retail, events.
- **Referral systems** — Systematic (not accidental) ways that existing customers bring new ones. Incentive structures, partnership programs, community-driven referrals.

#### 4. Product Assets
What you actually sell and how it's packaged.

- **Products for prospects (PFPs)** — Low-cost entry products that build trust and qualify leads (books, scorecards, workshops). Links to the ATM model.
- **Core products** — Your main revenue-generating offerings. Must be productised, consistent, and deliverable without you personally.
- **Products for clients (PFCs)** — Premium offerings for existing customers. High-value, high-touch.
- **Joint venture products** — Products created in partnership with complementary businesses. Extends reach and capability.

#### 5. Systems Assets
The operational infrastructure that makes delivery consistent and scalable.

- **Technology stack** — The software, tools, and platforms that run your business. CRM, email marketing, project management, accounting, automation.
- **Processes and SOPs** — Documented standard operating procedures for everything recurring. If only one person knows how to do something, it's not a system.
- **Automation** — Workflows that happen without human intervention. Email sequences, scheduling, invoicing, reporting, follow-ups.

#### 6. Culture Assets
The human infrastructure of your business.

- **Core team** — The key roles filled by people who are aligned, capable, and committed. (See core team section below.)
- **Advisors and mentors** — External wisdom you can access. Board members, advisory panels, peer groups.
- **Culture and values** — Documented principles that guide decisions, hiring, and behaviour. Not a poster on the wall — lived principles that people can point to when making choices.

#### 7. Funding Assets
Financial infrastructure and strategy.

- **Revenue model** — How money comes in. Recurring revenue is more valuable than one-off sales. Predictable income enables planning and investment.
- **Financial reporting** — Clear, current financial data. You can't manage what you can't measure. Investors and buyers look at this first.
- **Access to capital** — Relationships with banks, investors, grant bodies. Lines of credit established before you need them.

### Using the 24 Assets for diagnosis

The 24 Assets framework works as an audit tool. For each asset, rate the business 1–10:
- **1–3:** This asset barely exists or is very weak
- **4–6:** It exists but needs significant development
- **7–9:** Strong and contributing meaningfully to the business
- **10:** World-class, could stand on its own

The heat map reveals where the business is strong and where it's vulnerable. **Focus on developing 2–3 assets at a time** to world-class level before moving on. Trying to improve all 24 simultaneously leads to resource dilution.

**For established businesses feeling stuck:** The diagnosis almost always reveals that they've over-invested in a few asset categories (usually product and systems) while neglecting others (usually IP, brand, and market).

## The Core Team

From Oversubscribed. A campaign-driven, oversubscribed business needs four key roles. These can be filled by one person wearing multiple hats initially, but the roles must eventually be separated.

### 1. Key Person of Influence
Someone known, liked, and trusted in the industry. Sets the vision, defines the culture, has a powerful network. This is usually the founder.

**Responsibilities:**
- Industry visibility and relationship building
- Vision and strategy
- Partnership development
- Content creation and thought leadership

### 2. Head of Sales and Marketing
Manages lead generation, campaign execution, and conversion.

**Responsibilities:**
- Campaign planning and execution
- Lead generation and nurturing
- Sales conversations and closing
- Marketing asset creation
- CRM management and follow-up

### 3. Head of Operations and Production
Responsible for delivering remarkable results to customers.

**Responsibilities:**
- Product/service delivery
- Quality control
- Process improvement
- Customer experience
- Team management for delivery staff

### 4. Head of Finance, Logistics and Reporting
Keeps the numbers straight and ensures the business is financially sound.

**Responsibilities:**
- Financial tracking and reporting
- Cash flow management
- Purchasing and logistics
- Access to capital (loans, grants, investment)
- Data analysis and performance metrics

**Key insight:** Most small business owners try to fill all four roles themselves. This is the primary reason businesses plateau. The first hire that creates leverage is usually operations (so the founder can focus on KPI and sales/marketing).

## Business Stages

From 24 Assets. Understanding where a business sits on this journey helps diagnose what it needs next.

1. **Startup** — Excitement, ideas, planning. Small founding team laying groundwork.
2. **Wilderness** — Operating alone or near-alone. Resource-constrained, time-poor, limited freedom.
3. **Modest shop** — Small team but growth is capped. Revenue constrained by headcount and geography.
4. **Lifestyle boutique** — An influential director coordinates a skilled small team. Good income per person, high independence. Technology leveraged well.
5. **Desert** — A dangerous transition phase. Leadership changes, cultural shifts, funding needs. Many businesses die here trying to scale from boutique to factory.
6. **Factory/Production hub** — Larger team but earnings per person drop. Profits squeezed, talent retention becomes difficult.
7. **Performance** — Growth-focused, creative, strong support systems. Team is thriving and the business has momentum.
8. **Unicorn** — Rare. Massive scale, cultural icon status.

**Most Priestley readers should aim for lifestyle boutique or performance.** These are the stages where the business is profitable, enjoyable, and the founder has freedom.

## Lifestyle Business vs Performance Business

From Lifestyle Business Playbook. This is a crucial strategic choice.

### Lifestyle Business
Optimised for the founder's quality of life. Fun, freedom, and fulfilment.

**Characteristics:**
- Small, lean team (often < 10 people)
- High income per person
- Founder has significant time freedom
- Niche-dominant rather than market-dominant
- Technology and automation replace headcount
- Campaign-driven marketing (not constant hustle)
- Products and systems do the heavy lifting

**This is what most trades business owners, consultants, and service providers should aim for.** It's not "small" in a limiting sense — a lifestyle business can generate £500k+ revenue with 2–3 people and strong assets.

### Performance Business
Optimised for growth, impact, and eventual exit.

**Characteristics:**
- Larger team, more infrastructure
- Focused on scale and market share
- May involve external investment
- Building toward a liquidity event (sale, IPO)
- More complex operations and management layers

### How to decide

Ask: "What do I want my Tuesday morning to look like?" If the answer involves creative work, client relationships, and personal freedom — lifestyle. If the answer involves board meetings, investor relations, and building departments — performance.

Neither is better. But building the wrong one for your temperament leads to misery.

## Thinking in assets, not revenue

Priestley's fundamental mindset shift for established businesses: stop thinking exclusively about profit and loss. Start thinking about balance sheet.

**Revenue thinking:** "How do I make more sales this month?"
**Asset thinking:** "What can I build this month that will generate value for years?"

**Examples of the shift:**
- Instead of writing an email to one client → Write a guide that serves all clients forever (content asset)
- Instead of doing a custom proposal for every prospect → Create a productised offering with clear pricing (product asset)
- Instead of personally onboarding every customer → Document and automate the onboarding process (system asset)
- Instead of relying on word of mouth → Build a referral system with incentives (market asset)

**The 90-day test:** For everything you do, ask: "Would this still be generating value if I disappeared for 90 days?" If yes, you're building an asset. If no, you're just working.

## Coaching actions for scaling

1. **Run the 24 Assets audit** — Rate each of the 24 assets 1–10. Identify the 3 weakest that would have the biggest impact if strengthened.
2. **Clarify lifestyle vs performance** — Which type of business are they building? Are their current actions aligned with that choice?
3. **Map the core team** — Which of the four roles are covered? Which is the founder trying to do themselves that they shouldn't be?
4. **Identify the bottleneck** — Where is the founder the bottleneck? What would free them up? (Usually: productise the methodology, document processes, hire operations.)
5. **Apply the 90-day test** — Look at how they spend their time. How much is asset-building vs just working?
6. **Plan the next 2–3 asset builds** — Pick the highest-leverage assets and create a 90-day plan to build them.
7. **Review the product ecosystem** — Does the ATM exist? Is each level working? Where are the gaps?
