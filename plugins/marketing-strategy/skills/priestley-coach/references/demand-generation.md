# Demand Generation & Becoming Oversubscribed

This reference covers Priestley's frameworks for generating leads, creating demand, and building a business where customers line up to work with you.

## The core principle: Oversubscribed

A business is "oversubscribed" when demand exceeds its capacity to deliver. This is the only condition under which a business reliably makes profit. When supply exceeds demand, you're forced to discount, chase, and compromise.

**The critical number: Capacity**

Before any demand generation work, you must know your capacity — exactly how many customers you can serve remarkably in a given period. Not how many you could theoretically squeeze in. How many you can truly delight.

This number governs everything:
- How many leads you need
- When to open and close for new business
- When to raise prices
- When to hire

Most businesses skip this step and just try to "get more leads" without knowing what enough looks like. This leads to either underdelivering (too many clients, quality drops) or panic marketing (too few clients, desperate discounting).

**Action:** Define your capacity number. Be specific. "12 new clients per quarter" or "40 boiler installations per month" — not vague goals.

## The 7-11-4 Rule

This is Priestley's framework for understanding what it takes for someone to trust you enough to buy. Three research-backed thresholds:

### 7 Hours
Someone needs approximately 7 hours of interaction with you/your brand before they move from stranger to someone who feels they know you. This is the threshold where acquaintance-level awareness shifts to genuine trust.

**How to accumulate 7 hours:**
- Long-form content (books, podcast episodes, YouTube videos, workshops)
- Email sequences with genuine value
- Events (live or virtual)
- Free courses or challenge series
- Combination of all of the above

**Key insight:** This is why a book is so powerful. A reader might spend 5–8 hours with your ideas in a single sitting. That's most of the 7 hours done in one asset.

### 11 Touchpoints
Someone needs approximately 11 separate interactions with your brand before they're likely to buy. Each touchpoint is a distinct moment of contact.

**Touchpoints include:**
- Seeing a social media post
- Receiving an email
- Reading a blog post
- Watching a video
- Attending an event
- Hearing you on a podcast
- Being referred by a friend
- Seeing a piece of direct mail
- Taking a scorecard/quiz
- Visiting your website
- Reading your book

**Key insight:** This is why single-channel marketing fails. One email sequence alone won't get you to 11 touchpoints with enough frequency or variety.

### 4 Different Places
The trust effect is amplified when someone encounters you in at least 4 different contexts or platforms. Seeing someone only on Instagram feels one-dimensional. Seeing them on Instagram, in a book, at an event, and via email makes them feel omnipresent and legitimate.

**Places/contexts include:**
- Social media (each platform counts as a place)
- Email/newsletter
- A book or published content
- Live events or webinars
- Podcast (yours or as a guest)
- Physical mail
- Word of mouth / referral
- Your website
- Third-party media coverage

### Practical application of 7-11-4

The test: Could someone right now, today, binge on your free content for 7 hours, across 11 interactions, in 4 different places? If not, you have a content/presence gap to fill.

**Action:** Map your current 7-11-4 coverage. Where are the gaps? Which of the three numbers is weakest? Build content and presence to fill those gaps.

## The Campaign Driven Enterprise Model

Most businesses do "maintenance marketing" — the same recurring activities month after month. Priestley argues this is ineffective. Instead, businesses should operate as **campaign-driven enterprises** where marketing happens in structured, time-bound campaigns that create urgency and energy.

### Why campaigns beat maintenance

- Campaigns create a sense of event and momentum
- They give prospects a reason to act now, not later
- They let you connect with many people at once (one-to-many) rather than one at a time
- They create natural tension between supply and demand
- They're more fun for you and your team — campaigns have a rhythm and a finish line

### Campaign structure (5 phases)

#### Phase 1: Planning
- Define your capacity for this campaign period (e.g., this quarter)
- Identify your target market for this campaign
- Set a campaign theme (what story are you telling? what problem are you solving?)
- Build your campaign timeline with specific dates
- Prepare all assets you'll need (content, landing pages, emails, scorecard)

#### Phase 2: Build-up
- Signal to the market what you're planning
- Share content that educates and entertains around the campaign theme
- Open a waitlist or registration for interest
- Use products for prospects (workshops, scorecards, challenges) to engage people
- Collect signals of interest — who's raising their hand?
- This phase should feel like you're building anticipation, not selling

#### Phase 3: Release (only when oversubscribed)
- Once demand exceeds capacity, open for business
- Release in stages: early access for waitlist, then general availability
- Offer different tiers/packages/editions to capture different levels of commitment
- Use price rises and time limits to maintain urgency
- Be transparent about capacity ("12 spots remaining")

#### Phase 4: Delivery
- Deliver remarkably. This is your "remarkable budget" at work
- Exceed expectations. Create stories worth telling
- Collect testimonials, case studies, and data during delivery
- Create "energy up" moments — surprises, bonuses, celebrations

#### Phase 5: Celebrate and debrief
- Celebrate results with your team and clients
- Run a debrief: what worked? what didn't? what will you change?
- Collect stories, numbers, and insights for the next campaign
- Rest. Campaigns have a rhythm — there should be downtime between them

### Campaign rhythm

Priestley suggests running quarterly campaigns as a default rhythm. Four campaigns per year, each with a distinct theme and timeline. This creates predictability and allows for continuous improvement.

### Example quarterly campaign timeline

This is a practical template for a 12-week campaign cycle:

**Weeks 1–2: Planning**
- Define capacity for this quarter (how many clients/customers can you serve?)
- Choose campaign theme (seasonal angle, new product, industry trend, problem to solve)
- Prepare all assets: landing page, scorecard/quiz, email sequences, social content, event details
- Brief your team on roles and timeline

**Weeks 3–6: Build-up**
- Launch content around the campaign theme (blogs, videos, social posts, podcast episodes)
- Open waitlist or scorecard — collect signals of interest
- Run a "product for prospects" event (workshop, webinar, challenge) in week 5 or 6
- Nurture via email — educate, entertain, build trust
- Track signals: How many people are raising their hand?

**Weeks 7–8: Release**
- If signals exceed capacity: open for business, launch the offer
- If signals are below capacity: extend build-up, adjust messaging, or reduce capacity
- Release in stages: waitlist members get first access, then general availability
- Use transparency ("8 of 12 spots taken") and time limits to maintain urgency

**Weeks 9–10: Delivery**
- Deliver remarkably to the clients you've won
- Collect testimonials, case studies, and results data in real time
- Create "energy up" moments — surprises, bonuses, unexpected value

**Weeks 11–12: Celebrate and debrief**
- Celebrate with team and clients
- Run a formal debrief: What worked? What didn't? What changes for next quarter?
- Compile stories, numbers, and insights
- Rest. Plan seeds for the next campaign.

This template scales. A solopreneur can run a lighter version. A team of four (the core team) can run it at full intensity.

## Scorecard Marketing

From Scorecard Marketing (co-authored with Glen Carlson). This is Priestley's tactical lead generation methodology and ties directly into his ScoreApp software.

### The concept

A scorecard is an interactive quiz/assessment that lets prospects diagnose their own situation. They answer questions, receive a personalised score and report, and you receive their data and a qualified lead.

### Why scorecards work

- **Self-diagnosis beats being sold to.** People prefer discovering their own problems over being told they have them.
- **Data collection.** Every answer teaches you about the prospect's situation, needs, and readiness to buy.
- **Instant gratification.** The prospect gets a result immediately — a score, a diagnosis, a personalised report.
- **Warm leads.** Someone who's completed a 20-question assessment about their business is far warmer than someone who downloaded a generic PDF.
- **Scalable.** A scorecard runs 24/7 without you being involved.
- **7-11-4 contribution.** A scorecard interaction counts as a meaningful touchpoint.

### Four components of an effective scorecard

1. **Landing page** — Clear explanation of what the scorecard measures, who it's for, and what they'll get. Credibility markers (your experience, research basis, number of businesses served). Keep it focused — clarity, credibility, connection, then call to action.

2. **Questions** — Typically 15–25 questions organised into categories that map to your methodology. Questions should be thought-provoking, not obvious. They should educate the person about what matters even as they answer.

3. **Results page** — Personalised score with breakdown by category. Actionable insights. Clear next step.

4. **Follow-up sequence** — Automated emails that nurture based on the person's specific results and scores.

### Scorecard design principles

- **Base categories on your methodology** — If you have a 5-pillar framework, make those the 5 scoring categories
- **Questions should awaken dormant desires** — Priestley distinguishes between active desires (people already looking for solutions) and dormant desires (people who don't yet realise they have a problem). Great scorecards awaken dormant desires by asking questions that make people think "I hadn't considered that..."
- **Make the result feel like a consultation** — The output should be valuable enough on its own that people would pay for the insight
- **Use the data** — Segment your leads by their scores and tailor your follow-up accordingly. Someone scoring 2/10 needs different messaging than someone scoring 7/10

### Step-by-step: designing a scorecard

1. **Name it compellingly** — "How [adjective] is your [thing they care about]?" e.g. "How scalable is your trades business?" or "How healthy is your marketing?" The name should trigger curiosity about a topic they already care about.

2. **Choose 4–6 scoring categories** — These should map to the pillars of your methodology or the key areas where your expertise creates value. Each category gets 3–5 questions.

3. **Write questions that educate** — Every question should make the person think, not just collect data. Bad: "Do you have a website?" Good: "Does your website generate enquiries without you having to promote it?" The second question subtly teaches them what a good website should do.

4. **Design the results page** — Show an overall score plus a breakdown by category. Highlight their weakest area (this is where your service creates the most value). Include 2–3 actionable tips they can implement immediately — genuine value, not a teaser.

5. **Create the follow-up path** — Map each score range to a different email sequence. Low scorers need education about the problem. Mid scorers need proof you can solve it. High scorers need a reason to optimise further. All paths should naturally lead toward your core offering.

6. **Set up the landing page** — Clarity (what they'll learn), credibility (your experience/qualifications), connection (show you understand their world), call to action (take the scorecard). Mention how long it takes ("Less than 3 minutes for 20 questions").

## Signalling

Priestley's principle of "don't ask for the sale — ask for the signal" is critical throughout demand generation.

**Signals of interest include:**
- Joining a waitlist
- Completing a scorecard
- Registering for an event
- Downloading a resource
- Responding to an email
- Booking a discovery call

**Why signals matter:**
- They reduce risk for the prospect (low commitment)
- They give you data about demand levels
- They let you gauge readiness before investing in sales conversations
- Multiple signals from the same person indicate genuine intent

## The psychology of buying (from Scorecard Marketing)

People buy to resolve tension. Tension comes in two forms:

1. **Pain-based tension** — Something they have that they want to stop (problems, inefficiencies, losses)
2. **Desire-based tension** — Something they don't have that they want (growth, status, capability)

Great marketing identifies and amplifies the right tension, then offers resolution. Scorecards do this naturally — the questions surface tensions the person may not have consciously articulated.

**Active vs dormant desires:**
- **Active:** The person is already looking for a solution. These are few but ready to buy.
- **Dormant:** The person has the underlying need but hasn't articulated it yet. These are many but need awakening.

Most businesses only market to active desires (people already searching for their service). The huge opportunity is awakening dormant desires — Priestley argues this is far more lucrative because the pool of people is much larger and there's less competition for their attention.

## Coaching actions for demand generation

1. **Define capacity** — What's the specific number?
2. **Audit 7-11-4** — Map current content and presence against the three thresholds
3. **Fill the biggest 7-11-4 gap** — What's the quickest win? (Usually creating more long-form content for the 7-hour threshold)
4. **Build or improve a scorecard/PFP** — Create an interactive tool that generates warm leads
5. **Plan a campaign** — Structure the next quarter as a campaign with the 5 phases
6. **Set up signalling** — Create mechanisms to collect signals of interest (waitlist, scorecard, event registration)
7. **Review campaign rhythm** — Are they running campaigns or doing maintenance marketing?
