# New Business & Launch Framework

This reference covers Priestley's frameworks for people who are starting a new business, launching a new product/service, or validating an idea.

## The Entrepreneur Sweet Spot

Before anything else, a viable business idea sits at the intersection of three things:

1. **Something you're passionate about** — You'll need to sustain energy through hard times. If you don't care about it, you'll quit.
2. **Something you're good at** — You need genuine expertise or skill. Not hobbyist-level. Enough that people would pay for it.
3. **Something that makes money** — Passion and skill without a market is an expensive hobby. There must be people willing to pay.

If someone's idea is missing one of these three, flag it immediately. Help them adjust. The most common gap is #3 — people build something nobody wants to pay for because they didn't validate demand.

## Micro-niche selection

Priestley is emphatic: your "industry" is not a broad sector like "marketing" or "fitness" or "finance." It's a **micro-niche** — a specific enough segment that you can realistically dominate it within 12 months.

The test for a good micro-niche:
- You can become the go-to person in it within a year
- It's specific enough that when someone has that exact problem, your name comes up
- It's large enough to sustain a profitable business (but smaller than you think — the internet makes small niches viable)
- You can describe who it's for in one sentence

**Examples of bad niches → good micro-niches:**
- "Business coaching" → "Business coaching for trades business owners doing £250k–£600k who are drowning in admin"
- "Web design" → "Websites for estate agents who want to rank locally"
- "Financial planning" → "Financial planning for rural family farms"

Help the person narrow down relentlessly. Most people resist this because they're afraid of excluding potential customers. Priestley's counter: being the go-to person in a small pond creates far more opportunity than being invisible in the ocean.

## The Ascending Transaction Model (ATM)

This is from Entrepreneur Revolution and is one of Priestley's most practical frameworks. Every business needs four types of products that work together as an ecosystem:

### 1. Gifts (free, no strings)
Free products given to the world expecting nothing in return. No email gate, no strings. They demonstrate your brilliance and make people curious about what else you offer.

**Examples:** Blog posts, YouTube videos, podcast episodes, free tools, social media content, templates, checklists.

**Rules:**
- Must be genuinely valuable, not thinly-veiled sales pitches
- Should showcase your expertise and methodology
- Must be easy to find and share
- Create many of these — they're your content engine

### 2. Products for Prospects (PFP) — low cost, high value
These sit between free and your core offer. They let someone experience working with you at low risk. They build trust and qualify interest.

**Examples:** A book (£10–£20), a scorecard/quiz, a workshop (£50–£200), a mini-course, an audit, a diagnostic tool, a challenge.

**Rules:**
- Priced low enough that the purchase decision is easy
- Must deliver genuine transformation or insight
- Should naturally lead someone toward wanting your core offer
- Collects data and signals of interest
- This is where scorecard marketing fits perfectly

### 3. Core Offering
Your main product/service. The thing you're actually in business to sell. This is where most of your revenue comes from.

**Rules:**
- Priced at a level that makes your business profitable
- Delivered remarkably — this is what creates referrals and repeat business
- Must be productised enough that it's consistent and scalable (not completely bespoke every time)
- Should have clear outcomes the customer can expect

### 4. Products for Clients (PFC) — high-end, for existing customers
Premium offerings only available to people who've already bought your core offering. These deepen the relationship and increase lifetime value.

**Examples:** VIP programs, retainer services, masterminds, done-for-you upgrades, exclusive access, advisory boards.

**Rules:**
- Only offered to existing happy clients
- Higher price point, higher touch
- Creates a community of your best customers
- These clients become your biggest advocates

### The glue between levels

The ATM isn't just four products sitting in a list. The glue between each level matters:

- **Gift → PFP:** Content that naturally educates someone toward realising they need a deeper assessment/experience (your PFP)
- **PFP → Core:** The product for prospects should diagnose a problem or reveal an opportunity that your core offering solves
- **Core → PFC:** Remarkable delivery of your core offering should make clients want to go deeper

## Validating with a Waitlist

Priestley strongly advocates validating demand before building anything:

1. **Describe the offer** — Write a clear description of what you're planning to create
2. **Create a waitlist** — Use a simple landing page asking people to register interest
3. **Signal your intentions** — Tell your network what you're planning and invite them to join the waitlist
4. **Measure response** — The number of waitlist signups tells you whether to proceed, adjust, or abandon
5. **Engage the waitlist** — Ask questions, gather feedback, refine the offer based on real responses
6. **Launch to the waitlist first** — Give waitlist members priority access, early-bird pricing, or bonuses

This is Priestley's "signal before sell" principle in action. A waitlist is a low-commitment way for people to say "I'm interested" without you having to build the thing first.

**Key insight:** If you can't get people to join a free waitlist, you definitely can't get them to pay. This saves enormous amounts of wasted time and money.

## Seven hard truths (from Entrepreneur Revolution)

When coaching someone starting out, be honest about these. Priestley doesn't sugar-coat entrepreneurship:

1. It's hard and it gets harder
2. No one is coming to save you
3. In order to do the work you love, you have to win the work
4. Things don't work for long — you'll need to keep adapting
5. You are going to fail at some things
6. It's unfair — some people will copy you, undercut you, or get lucky
7. You're not entitled to rewards — the market decides

These aren't reasons not to start. They're reasons to go in with realistic expectations and build real assets rather than hoping for shortcuts.

## ATM in practice — example patterns

### Trades consultancy (e.g. helping plumbing businesses get organised)
- **Gifts:** YouTube videos showing common admin disasters and how to fix them. Blog posts on "5 signs your plumbing business is running you."
- **PFP:** A scorecard — "How organised is your plumbing business?" (scores them across job management, invoicing, scheduling, customer communication). £29 workshop: "The 90-minute plumbing business health check."
- **Core:** 12-week implementation programme setting up their systems, processes, and automations. £3,000–£5,000.
- **PFC:** Monthly retainer for ongoing optimisation, quarterly business reviews, access to a private community. £500/month.

### Marketing agency (e.g. SEO for local service businesses)
- **Gifts:** Free local SEO audit tool. Blog posts on "Why your competitors show up on Google Maps and you don't."
- **PFP:** A book — "The Local Business Owner's Guide to Getting Found Online." A scorecard — "How visible is your business online?" £97 half-day workshop on local marketing fundamentals.
- **Core:** Managed local SEO service — monthly retainer for content, Google Business Profile management, and website optimisation. £800–£2,000/month.
- **PFC:** Done-for-you website rebuild. Google Ads management bolt-on. Quarterly strategy sessions. Annual marketing planning day.

### Business coach / consultant
- **Gifts:** Podcast, newsletter, LinkedIn content sharing frameworks and insights.
- **PFP:** A book codifying the methodology. A scorecard assessing business readiness. £149 diagnostic workshop.
- **Core:** 6-month coaching programme or accelerator. £5,000–£15,000.
- **PFC:** Mastermind group. Annual retreat. 1-to-1 advisory retainer.

## Coaching actions for new businesses

When someone is at the idea/launch stage, walk them through these in order:

1. **Validate the sweet spot** — Does this idea sit at the intersection of passion, skill, and market?
2. **Define the micro-niche** — Who specifically is this for? Can you dominate this niche in 12 months?
3. **Design the ATM** — What would each of the four product levels look like?
4. **Start with gifts** — What free content can you create immediately to demonstrate expertise?
5. **Build a PFP** — What low-cost product for prospects would let people experience your value? (A scorecard or workshop is often the fastest to create)
6. **Run a waitlist** — Before building the core offering, validate demand with a waitlist
7. **Launch to the waitlist** — Use the Campaign Driven Enterprise model (see demand-generation.md) to run your launch
