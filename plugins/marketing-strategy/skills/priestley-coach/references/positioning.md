# Positioning & Personal Brand Framework

This reference covers Priestley's Key Person of Influence methodology for becoming the go-to authority in your micro-niche, plus de-commoditisation strategies.

## What is a Key Person of Influence?

A Key Person of Influence (KPI) is someone who sits at the centre of their industry's inner circle — widely known, trusted, and highly valued. KPIs attract opportunities rather than chasing them. Their names come up in conversation for the right reasons.

The critical distinction: the difference between a struggling expert and a high-earning KPI isn't talent. It's **the assets they own.** Talent is tied to your time. Assets — a sharp pitch, a published book, a product ecosystem, a strong profile — work for you around the clock.

## The KPI 5-Step Method

These five steps build on each other. Each creates a tangible asset that compounds your influence.

### 1. Pitch

Your pitch is a concise, compelling explanation of who you help, what problem you solve, and why you're the person to solve it. It should spark curiosity and open doors.

**A good pitch:**
- Can be delivered in under 60 seconds
- Makes the listener immediately think of someone who needs you
- Communicates unique value, not generic capability
- Creates a "tell me more" reaction

**Common mistakes:**
- Describing what you do instead of the problem you solve
- Being too broad ("I help businesses grow")
- Using jargon that only insiders understand
- Failing to differentiate from everyone else who does similar work

**Action:** Write your pitch. Test it on 10 people this week. If they don't immediately get it, rewrite it. Keep iterating until strangers can repeat back what you do.

### 2. Publish

Publishing establishes credibility and thought leadership. It makes your ideas tangible and shareable. In Priestley's world, publishing a book is the gold standard — but it's not the only form.

**Forms of publishing (ascending impact):**
- Blog posts, articles, social media content
- Podcast (hosting or guesting)
- White papers, guides, reports
- A book (this is the big one — Priestley considers it essential for KPIs)

**Why a book matters so much:**
- It positions you as the authority, not just another practitioner
- It forces you to organise your methodology and thinking
- It's a product for prospects (ATM level 2) that works as a business card on steroids
- It creates 7-11-4 content automatically (people spend hours with your book across a different "place")

**Action:** If you haven't published a book, this should be a priority. If a full book feels too big, start with a substantial guide or report that codifies your methodology.

### 3. Product

Turn your expertise into scalable products. This links directly to the Ascending Transaction Model (see new-business.md). The key shift: move from selling your time to selling your intellectual property in product form.

**Productisation means:**
- Your methodology is documented and repeatable
- Value delivery doesn't require you personally every time
- The product can be sold at a consistent price regardless of who's delivering it
- You can serve more people without working more hours

**Common resistance:** "But my work is bespoke — every client is different." Priestley's response: there is always a repeatable methodology underneath. The 80% is systematisable. The 20% is genuinely custom. Productise the 80%.

### 4. Profile

Build a strong, recognisable presence that positions you as the leading authority. This is your visibility strategy.

**Profile components:**
- Professional online presence (website, LinkedIn, relevant social platforms)
- Speaking engagements (conferences, podcasts, panels)
- Media mentions and features
- Awards and recognitions
- Association with other respected figures in your niche

**Important:** Profile is about strategic visibility, not vanity metrics. 1,000 followers in your exact micro-niche who trust and respect you beats 100,000 random followers. Priestley himself has modest social media followings relative to his influence — his profile is concentrated where it matters.

### 5. Partnership

Collaborate strategically to expand reach and credibility. Partnerships multiply your influence faster than solo efforts.

**Types of valuable partnerships:**
- Joint ventures with complementary businesses
- Referral partnerships
- Co-created content or products
- Strategic alliances with industry bodies
- Relationships with other KPIs in adjacent niches

**The partnership test:** Does this partnership give both parties access to an audience or capability they don't currently have? If it's one-sided, it won't last.

## De-commoditisation

If your offering looks the same as your competitors', you're a commodity. Commodities compete on price, which destroys margins and creates a race to the bottom.

**Priestley's de-commoditisation strategy:**

1. **Have a unique methodology** — Give your process a name. Document it. Make it yours. "The [Your Name] Method" immediately separates you from generic practitioners.

2. **Lead with a personal brand** — People trust people, not logos. The founder or key person being known, liked, and trusted in the industry creates differentiation that can't be copied.

3. **Create remarkable experiences** — Replace your marketing budget with a "remarkable budget." Spend money making your delivery so good people can't help talking about it.

4. **Tell stories, not features** — Commodities list features. Differentiated businesses tell stories about transformation, philosophy, and impact.

5. **Be separate from the market** — If you link your business to the industry, the market, and the trends everyone else follows, you compete on price. Instead, create your own category or frame of reference.

6. **Name your terms** — Be willing to disqualify prospects and fire bad customers. This sounds counterintuitive, but it signals confidence and scarcity.

## Vital vs Functional

Priestley distinguishes between two modes of operating:

**Functional people** follow processes, do what's expected, and maintain the status quo. They're competent but interchangeable.

**Vital people** create value, drive innovation, attract opportunity, and are re-energised by their work. They are irreplaceable because they generate energy and ideas.

Being vital isn't about personality type. It's about choosing to show up as a creator and leader rather than a processor and follower. A KPI must be vital.

**Signs you're being functional when you need to be vital:**
- You're doing the same things this month as last month
- You're reacting to what comes in rather than creating opportunities
- You haven't created anything new recently
- Your energy is depleted rather than generated by your work
- Customers see you as "one of several options" rather than "the one I want"

## Coaching actions for positioning

1. **Audit current positioning** — Where do they sit? Are they a commodity or differentiated? Can they clearly articulate who they serve and what makes them different?
2. **Define the micro-niche** — If they're too broad, narrow down until they can realistically dominate
3. **Craft the pitch** — Write it, test it, iterate
4. **Identify the publishing gap** — What should they publish? What form? What timeline?
5. **Map the productisation opportunity** — What knowledge/process in their head can become a product?
6. **Plan the profile strategy** — Where do their ideal customers look for expertise? Be there.
7. **Identify partnership opportunities** — Who serves the same audience with complementary services?
